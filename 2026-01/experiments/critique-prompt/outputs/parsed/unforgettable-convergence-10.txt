"The Meta-Ethical Confidence Problem" attacks the paper's practice of drawing conclusions by conditioning on different metaethical views. The authors argue that given realism, X follows; given antirealism, Y follows; therefore we should be pessimistic. But this inference pattern only works if our uncertainty across metaethical views is well-calibrated. If we're systematically overconfident in antirealism (perhaps because it's culturally dominant in academic philosophy), then the paper's pessimistic conclusions are driven by that miscalibration rather than by the object-level considerations.
